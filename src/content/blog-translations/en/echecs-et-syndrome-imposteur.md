You have just won a well-played game. Your opponent was 100 points above you. The win is clear and deserved. Yet the first thought that surfaces is not satisfaction. It is something more uncomfortable: *they must have blundered. It doesn't really count. If we replayed, they would win.*

You may know this feeling: the inability to own a victory, to feel it as legitimate. That persistent doubt about your own level even when the numbers say otherwise. That sense that your Elo rises by accident, that others do not yet know you are not really good.

This is not modesty. It is not realism. It is impostor syndrome. And in chess it takes particularly insidious forms.

> **The essentials in 4 points:**
> - Impostor syndrome is a well-documented psychological phenomenon, especially common in intellectual and competitive activities
> - In chess it often shows up as wins attributed to the opponent's luck and losses taken as proof of your "true" level
> - The nature of the Elo system makes things worse: a number that goes up and down creates an unstable player identity tied to results
> - The solution is not to "believe in yourself" more, but to change the frame you use to judge your own progress

## What impostor syndrome really is

### The original definition

The term was introduced by psychologists [Pauline Clance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauline_Clance) and Suzanne Imes in 1978 in a study of high-achieving women graduates who still felt incompetent despite objective evidence of success. They named this **"impostor phenomenon"**: the feeling of being a fraud who will sooner or later be exposed.

What matters in the original definition: it is not ordinary lack of confidence. It is a specific split between real performance and how you attribute that performance internally. People affected tend to:

- **Attribute successes to external factors**: luck, opponent errors, a favourable position
- **Attribute failures to internal factors**: lack of talent, insufficient level, your "true nature"
- **Anticipate exposure**: the belief that eventually others will see your displayed level as fake

This attribution pattern is the opposite of what a balanced evaluation would produce.

### Who is affected?

Decades of research have broadened the definition far beyond Clance and Imes's initial sample. A meta-analysis by [Bravata et al. (2020)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jpsychires.2019.01.016) covering 62 studies and over 14,000 participants estimates that **70% of people** will experience at least one significant episode of impostor syndrome at some point in life.

The profiles most at risk are those in highly intellectual, highly competitive fields with visible, objective criteria. Researchers, engineers, doctors, and chess players tick all those boxes.

## Why chess makes it worse

### Elo as an unforgiving mirror

The [Elo system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elo_rating_system) was designed as a neutral statistical measure of relative performance. In practice it becomes, for many players, an identity: *I am a 1450 player, I am an 1800 player.*

That slip is problematic because Elo fluctuates. A run of bad games (fatigue, tilt, opponents in great form) drags the rating down. For someone prone to impostor syndrome that drop does not feel like normal variance. It feels like confirmation: *there is my real level. Before was a bluff.*

Conversely, a good run that pushes Elo up does not produce a sense of legitimacy. It produces anxiety: *expectations are higher now. I cannot keep this up.*

Elo creates a loop where it is hard to find a stable footing. There is no "good moment" to feel legitimate: when it rises, it is temporary; when it falls, it is reality.

### The total visibility of error

In chess every mistake can be proved objectively. After a game [Stockfish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stockfish_(chess)) can show you, move by move, where you played badly and what you should have played. There is nowhere to hide.

In other intellectual or sporting domains error can be nuanced, debated, contextualised. In chess it is codified: -0.8 centipawns, that was an error. The engine said so.

For someone whose cognitive filter amplifies failures and minimises successes, post-game analysis can turn into self-flagellation. You remember the blunders. You skip past the good moves. The conclusion is always the same: *I play badly.*

### The scene itself

Chess culture does not help. A scene where 12-year-olds beat experienced adults, ratings are public and comparable, and the vocabulary itself ranks people (*amateur, semi-pro, titled, GM*) is an environment built for downward comparison.

Downward comparison is one of impostor syndrome's main fuels: you always compare yourself to those who play better, never to those you surpass. In chess there is always someone who plays better.

[Magnus Carlsen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnus_Carlsen) has said in several interviews that he felt intense pressure around his performances at the top, a sense that every game below his absolute peak was evidence of decline. If the strongest player in history can feel something like impostor syndrome, the phenomenon is not reserved for modest ratings.

## Concrete manifestations

### "My wins don't count"

The classic form: the inability to validate a win. Your opponent was tired. They played a line they did not know. I got lucky on the clock. They were having a bad day.

There is always an external explanation for a win when you have impostor syndrome. Sometimes it is even true: chess wins always involve some luck and opponent error. The issue is not the explanation itself, but that it applies systematically to wins and never to losses.

### "I will be found out"

This pattern hits especially players who improve fast. Gaining 200 or 300 points in a few months creates specific anxiety: the feeling that you do not "deserve" that rating, that it rests on a favourable stretch that must correct itself.

That anticipation of failure can paradoxically cause it. Sports psychology speaks of a **self-fulfilling prophecy**: fear of exposure creates hypervigilance about your own errors, tension in the game that hurts quality, and confirmation of the original fears.

### "I don't really understand what I'm doing"

This is the intellectual version, very common in chess: the feeling of playing correctly without really knowing why. Good moves "by intuition" without being able to explain them. Applying memorised principles without having deeply internalised them.

That feeling has a grain of truth: understanding in chess is graded, and you can play correct moves for partly correct reasons. But it is blown out of proportion for players prone to impostor syndrome. The incompleteness of understanding (normal and universal at every level) is read as proof of fraud rather than as a normal stage of development.

## What does not work as a solution

### "You need to believe in yourself"

The usual advice is useless or harmful. Impostor syndrome is not a confidence gap fixed by positive thinking. It is a cognitive attribution pattern with its own mechanics. Telling someone "believe in yourself more" without changing those mechanics is like telling a depressed person to "be happier."

### Stacking wins to prove your level

The intuition is that if you win enough, impostor feelings will vanish. In practice they do not. Each win gets reclassified by the impostor filter: *that was easy, it doesn't count, they were below their level.* The bar keeps moving.

Studies of impostor phenomenon among academics show that the most accomplished people (those with the strongest objective evidence of skill) sometimes suffer the most intense syndrome. Performance alone does not cure it.

## What does work

### Change the metric

The first concrete step is to **shift evaluation from outcome to process**. Instead of "did I win or lose?", the question becomes "did I apply what I am learning?" or "did I handle the middlegame phase well in this game?"

This draws directly on [Carol Dweck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carol_Dweck)'s work on growth mindset versus fixed mindset. A player in a fixed mindset treats each game as a measure of innate talent. A player in a growth mindset treats it as information about current development.

For impostor syndrome that shift matters: if evaluation targets process (did I analyse this position well? did I resist rushing in zeitnot?), wins and losses become data, not identity verdicts.

### Externalise progress tracking

Keeping a game journal with concrete observations (not "I played well" or "I played badly" but "I spotted the threat at move 23" or "I missed the knight fork because I did not calculate three moves deep") builds a personal dataset that impostor syndrome cannot easily rewrite.

Objectifying progress with concrete data resists cognitive distortions better than a vague impression. It is harder to say "I have not improved" when you have a month-by-month list of tactics you have mastered.

### Normalise uncertainty

A core piece of impostor syndrome is the belief that others *know* what they are doing, that their understanding is complete where yours is patchy. That is almost always false.

Grandmasters describing their in-game decision-making often admit unexplained intuition, "it felt right though I could not say exactly why." [Garry Kasparov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garry_Kasparov) wrote that some of his best moves were played before they were fully calculated. Uncertainty and partial intuition are not signs of incompetence; they are part of the game at every level.

### Talk to other players

Isolation helps keep impostor syndrome in place. The belief that you alone feel illegitimate does not survive an honest conversation with other players.

In clubs, forums, and online communities, stories of players who feel "fake" despite their rating are common. Discovering that feeling is shared (by players at every level, including those you admire) takes some of its emotional charge away.

**In summary**

| Manifestation | What makes it worse | What helps |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "My wins are luck" | Post-game analysis focused only on errors | Keep a process-oriented journal, not a results journal |
| "I will be exposed" | Fast improvement without objective anchors | Identify concrete criteria for progress |
| "I don't really understand" | Comparing yourself to stronger players | Normalise uncertainty; it is universal |
| Unstable Elo identity | Treating normal fluctuation as verdicts | Shift evaluation from rating to development |

## Frequently asked questions

**Can impostor syndrome lower your playing strength?**

Yes, through several mechanisms. Hypervigilance about your own errors creates tension that hurts calculation. Fear of "confirming" a low level can make you too cautious or push you to take excessive risks to "prove" something. The mental load of negative self-evaluation eats cognitive resources you need for the game.

**Does it go away as you improve?**

Not automatically. Some players report the syndrome easing with experience and consistency. Others find it moves: at 1200 you feel like an impostor against 1400s; at 1800 against 2000s. Improvement alone is not enough; you also need to change the evaluation frame.

**How do you tell impostor syndrome from a realistic view of your level?**

That is a fair question. Someone who overestimates their level is not experiencing impostor syndrome. The key is attribution pattern: if you systematically credit successes to external factors and failures to internal ones, that is a hallmark signal. If your assessment varies by game without a systematic pattern, it is more likely normal calibration.

**Do professional players feel this too?**

Yes, and they talk about it more openly. Fabiano Caruana has mentioned in interviews the pressure of maintaining a high rating and the feeling of not "deserving" certain positions in elite tournaments. Normalising that discourse among professionals is recent but meaningful.

**Should you see a professional?**

If impostor syndrome spills into other areas of life (work, relationships, other activities) and causes significant distress, working with a cognitive-behavioural therapist can help. CBT is especially effective on negative attribution patterns. In a purely chess context, the practices described here are usually enough.
